How TV networks are dealing with the novel coronavirus this fall
By Jessica Lerner
Like so many others in the arts sector, the novel coronavirus has had a substantial impact on the television industry. Upon the beginning of restrictions and stay-at-home orders last spring, production was either shut down or delayed, forcing networks to become dependent on their remaining inventory. This resulted in the the delay of some completed programs to air as contingencies in place of other programs, as well as forcing many shows to air ersatz season finales for series whose production was interrupted. And thanks to the still-raging pandemic, production of scripted television remains largely delayed throughout the country, making it impossible for networks to premiere most of their shows -- both new and old -- in late September and early October. Facing the reality networks won't have new episodes to air until at least late October or early November, executives had to get creative this fall to fill the gaps.
Heavier reliance on more acquisitions and imported content
One way broadcast networks are bridging the gaps are by relying on imported shows, from both other platforms and countries. CBS will pull from ViacomCBS's existing programs, airing “Star Trek: Discovery,” from the CBS All Access streaming site, and “One Day at a Time,” a comedy reboot that originated on Netflix but was canceled before moving to the ViacomCBS-owned cable network Pop. Meanwhile, Fox has acquired “L.A.’s Finest,” a crime drama that originally aired on the Spectrum cable system. Networks also went beyond U.S. borders to find content. Two examples: The NBC medical drama “Transplant” is a Canadian production, and The CW has “Devils,” a financial thriller made in Italy.
A rise in non-scripted productions
Networks have also upped their non-scripted content, as compared with scripted programming, producers can turn around new episodes of reality shows, talent competitions, and game shows much faster. CBS will continue airing nightly the second season of "Love Island," and "Big Brother," a summertime affair that is filling three different time slots for the network. ABC will also lean heavily on unscripted fare, with returning staples "Dancing With the Stars," “Shark Tank,” and “The Bachelorette." Additionally, singing competitions will be back in full force with NBC airing "The Voice" and Fox airing "The Masked Singer."
Fall sports
The return of sports is a coup for TV networks, as live sports account for the majority of the most-watched broadcasts every year, only growing more important as more viewers have switched to streaming services for content. This fall, sports fans will be inundated with choices. Never before have competitions in all the major U.S. sports leagues -- football, basketball, baseball, soccer, hockey, tennis, and golf -- happened at the same time. Along with golf and tennis, The National Basketball Association and National Hockey League are staging their championships in the fall for the first time.
